Geometric Algebra

Job Feldbrugge

Geometric algebra is an extension of vector algebra introducing the notion of a multi-vector.

Two-dimensional geometric algebra

As an example, consider the two-dimensional geometric algebra spanned by the vectors \(\hat{\textbf{x}}\) and \(\hat{\textbf{y}}\). The geometric algebra is spanned by the elements \(\{1, \hat{\textbf{x}}, \hat{\textbf{y}}, \hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\}\) where \(1\) is the unit scalar, \(\hat{\textbf{x}}\) and \(\hat{\textbf{y}}\) are the unit vectors and \(\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\) is the unique bi-vector representing an oriented area element. A multi-vector is an object of the form \(\textbf{v} = a + b \hat{\textbf{x}} + c \hat{\textbf{y}} + d \hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\) with for example real numbers \(a,b,c,d\). The product of two multi-vectors is defined by the rules \(\hat{\textbf{x}}^2=\hat{\textbf{y}}^2=1\) and \(\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}=-\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\). The geometric product of two vectors takes the form \[\begin{align} \textbf{v} \textbf{w} &= (a_v \hat{\textbf{x}} + b_v \hat{\textbf{y}})(a_w \hat{\textbf{x}} + b_w \hat{\textbf{y}})\\ &=(a_v a_w + b_v b_w) + (a_v b_w - b_v a_w)\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\\ &=\textbf{a}\cdot\textbf{b} + \textbf{a}\wedge\textbf{b}\ \hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}} \end{align}\] where we identify the inner and outer products. Now since \[\begin{align} (\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}})^2&=\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\\ &=-\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\hat{\textbf{x}}\\ &=-\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{x}}\\ &=-1 \end{align}\] it is common to identify \(\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\) with the square root of minus one \(i=\sqrt{-1}\) and write the geometric product of two vectors as the complex number \(\textbf{v} \textbf{w} =\textbf{a}\cdot\textbf{b} + \textbf{a}\wedge\textbf{b}\). In the two-dimensional setting, geometric algebra unifies vector analysis with complex analysis. Note that the geometric product of multi-vectors, unlike the inner or outer products, is invertible, i.e., \[ \textbf{a}^{-1} = \textbf{a}/\|\textbf{a}\|\] with the norm \(\|\textbf{a}\|^2=\textbf{a}^2\).

The geometric product of two general multi-vectors takes the form \[\begin{align} \textbf{v} \textbf{w} &= (a_v + b_v \hat{\textbf{x}} + c_v \hat{\textbf{y}} + d_v \hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}) (a_w + b_w \hat{\textbf{x}} + c_w \hat{\textbf{y}} + d_w \hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}})\\ &= (a_v a_w + b_v b_w + c_v c_w - d_v d_w) + \\ &\phantom{=}(a_v b_w + b_v a_w - c_v d_w + d_v c_w )\hat{\textbf{x}} + (a_v c_w + c_v a_w + b_v d_w - d_v b_w)\hat{\textbf{y}} + \\ &\phantom{=}(a_v d_w + b_v c_w - c_v b_w+ d_v a_w)\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}} \end{align}\]

Three-dimensional geometric algebra

The three-dimensional geometric algebra is spanned by the elements \(\{1, \hat{\textbf{{x}}}, \hat{\textbf{{y}}}, \hat{\textbf{{z}}}, \hat{\textbf{{x}}} \hat{\textbf{{y}}}, \hat{\textbf{{x}}} \hat{\textbf{{z}}}, \hat{\textbf{{y}}} \hat{\textbf{{z}}}, \hat{\textbf{{x}}} \hat{\textbf{{y}}}\hat{\textbf{{z}}}\}\) consisting of the unit scalar, three vectors corresponding to the three independent directions, three bi-vectors corresponding to the three independent two-dimensional hyperplanes, and a unique tri-vector corresponding to the volume element. The product of two vectors takes the form \[\begin{align} \textbf{v}\textbf{w} &= (a_v \hat{\textbf{x}}+b_v \hat{\textbf{y}}+c_v \hat{\textbf{x}}) (a_w \hat{\textbf{x}}+b_w \hat{\textbf{y}}+c_w \hat{\textbf{x}})\\ &= (a_v a_w + b_v b_w + c_v c_w) + (a_v b_w - b_v a_w) \hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}+ (a_v c_w - c_v a_w)\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{z}}+ (b_v c_w - c_v b_w)\hat{\textbf{y}}\hat{\textbf{z}} \end{align}\] where we again can identify the inner and outer products of the vectors \(\textbf{v}\) and \(\textbf{w}\). We again write this product of vectors as \(\textbf{v}\textbf{w} = \textbf{v}\cdot \textbf{w} + \textbf{v} \wedge \textbf{w}\). The bi-vectors \(\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}},\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{z}},\hat{\textbf{y}}\hat{\textbf{z}}\) are again roots of unity and can be identified with the quaternions \(\textbf{i}, \textbf{j},\) and \(\textbf{k}\). The tri-vector \(\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\hat{\textbf{z}}\) is also a root of unity, i.e., \((\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\hat{\textbf{z}})^2=-1\). The quaternions are dual to the vectors. This is a special property of the three-dimensional geometric algebra.
