Geometric Algebra

Job Feldbrugge

Geometric algebra is an extension of vector algebra introducing the notion of a multi-vector. As an example, consider the two dimensional geometric algebra spanned by the vectors \(\hat{\textbf{x}}\) and \(\hat{\textbf{y}}\). The geometric algebra is spanned by the elements \(\{1, \hat{\textbf{x}}, \hat{\textbf{y}}, \hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\}\) where \(1\) is the unit scalar, \(\hat{\textbf{x}}\) and \(\hat{\textbf{y}}\) are the unit vectors and \(\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\) is the unique bivector representing an oriented area element. A multi-vector is an object of the form \(\textbf{v} = a + b \hat{\textbf{x}} + c \hat{\textbf{y}} + d \hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\) with for example real numbers \(a,b,c,d\). The product of two multi-vectors is defined by the rules \(\hat{\textbf{x}}^2=\hat{\textbf{y}}^2=1\) and \(\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}=-\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\). The geometric product of two vectors takes the form \[\begin{align} \textbf{v} \textbf{w} &= (a_v \hat{\textbf{x}} + b_v \hat{\textbf{y}})(a_w \hat{\textbf{x}} + b_w \hat{\textbf{y}})\\ &=(a_v a_w + b_v b_w) + (a_v b_w - b_v a_w)\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\\ &=\textbf{a}\cdot\textbf{b} + \textbf{a}\wedge\textbf{b}\ \hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}} \end{align}\] where we identify the inner and outer products. Now since \[\begin{align} (\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}})^2&=\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\\ &=-\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\hat{\textbf{x}}\\ &=-\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{x}}\\ &=-1 \end{align}\] it is common to identify \(\hat{\textbf{x}}\hat{\textbf{y}}\) with the square root of minus one \(i=\sqrt{-1}\) and write the geometric product of two vectors as the complex number \(\textbf{v} \textbf{w} =\textbf{a}\cdot\textbf{b} + \textbf{a}\wedge\textbf{b}\ i\). Note that the geometric product of multi-vectors, unlike the inner or outer products, are invertible, i.e., \[ \textbf{a}^{-1} = \textbf{a}/\|\textbf{a}\|\] with the norm \(\|\textbf{a}\|^2=\textbf{a}^2\).
